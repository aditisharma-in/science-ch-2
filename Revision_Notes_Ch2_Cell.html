How to Study Cells?

ConceptKey Point
ResolutionAbility of human eye to see two close objects as separate — 0.1 mm at 25 cm distance
MicroscopeUses convex lens / combination of lenses (objective + eyepiece) to magnify objects
Light MicroscopeUses visible light; common in school labs; objective lenses 10X, 40X
Electron MicroscopeUses beam of electrons instead of light; reveals nanometre-scale details
Cell size estimateFormula: Diameter of field (μm) ÷ No. of cells along diameter

Magnification = Eyepiece power × Objective lens power
> e.g., 10X × 10X = 100X total magnification

Unit conversion: 1 mm = 1000 μm | 1 nm = 0.000001 mm

Robert Hooke — First observed cell in 1665 using self-designed microscope (~200–300X). Observed box-like compartments in cork and named them "cells".
A student sees 20 onion cells across a microscope field of diameter 4000 μm. What is the size of one onion cell? If eyepiece is 10X and objective is 40X, how large will the cell appear?
Show Answer
Size = 4000/20 = 200 μm. Total magnification = 10 × 40 = 400X. Apparent size = 200 × 400 = 80,000 μm = 80 mm.

Cell Membrane — Universal Feature of All Cells

  • Thin boundary surrounding the cell — protects contents
  • Also called plasma membrane
  • Selectively permeable — allows some substances, blocks others

Osmosis

Movement of water through a selectively permeable membrane from dilute → concentrated solution

Potato experiment (Activity 2.2):

BeakerSolutionPotato piece
APlain waterSwells (water enters)
B20% salt/sugarShrinks (water leaves)

Effect of Solutions on Cells

SolutionExtracellular vs IntracellularEffect
HypotonicOutside < Inside concentrationCell swells (water enters)
HypertonicOutside > Inside concentrationCell shrinks (water leaves)
IsotonicOutside = Inside concentrationNo change

Structure of Cell Membrane (Fluid-Mosaic Model)

  • Lipid bilayer — 2 layers of fat molecules (heads outward, tails inward)
  • Proteins embedded — act as gatekeepers
  • Fluid — molecules move sideways, flip, rotate
  • Mosaic — molecules arranged like tiles
  • Thickness: 7–10 nm
A student places a Rhoeo leaf peel in 20% sugar solution. After 30 min, the inner contents shrink but cell shape remains same. Explain why.
Show Answer
Water moves out due to osmosis (hypertonic solution). The rigid cell wall maintains the outer shape while the cell membrane pulls away from it. Animal cells (no cell wall) would shrink entirely.

Cell Wall — Outer Covering (Plants, Fungi, Bacteria)

FeatureCell MembraneCell Wall
PermeabilitySelectively permeableFully permeable
Present inAll cellsPlants, fungi, bacteria only
CompositionLipids + proteinsCellulose (carbohydrate)
FunctionGatekeeperRigidity, structural support
  • Plant cells: box-shaped, regularly arranged
  • Animal cells: irregularly shaped, flexible (no cell wall)
  • Cellulose in our diet acts as roughage
Predict what would happen if a plant cell's cell wall became as flexible as the cell membrane.
Show Answer
The plant cell would lose its rigid shape, could not stand upright against wind/rain, leaves and stems would collapse, and the plant would wilt easily. It could not maintain firm structure.

Cell Interior — Organelles

All eukaryotic cells have three basic parts:

  1. Plasma membrane — selectively permeable
  2. Cytoplasm — semi-fluid, jelly-like substance
  3. Nucleus — prominent, well-defined

4.1 Nucleus — House of Coded Instructions

PartFunction
Nuclear membraneDouble-layered, has pores for material transfer
NucleolusDense round body; site of ribosomal subunit synthesis
ChromatinEntangled thread-like DNA (in non-dividing cell)
ChromosomesRod-shaped (visible during cell division); contain DNA + proteins
GenesFunctional segments of DNA that carry inheritance info

From cell to DNA: Cell → Nucleus → Chromosomes → DNA → Genes

Threads of Curiosity

Mature human RBCs have no nucleus (enucleate) — allows more space for haemoglobin. Lifespan ~120 days. Cannot repair/divide.

Prokaryotic cells: DNA as single circular molecule in nucleoid region (no membrane around it).

Why do mature mammalian RBCs lack a nucleus? What disadvantage does this cause?
Show Answer
Absence of nucleus provides more space for haemoglobin → transports more oxygen. Disadvantage: cannot repair or divide, so lifespan is short (~120 days).

4.2 Ribosomes — Protein Factories

  • Sites of protein synthesis
  • Present freely in cytoplasm OR attached to Rough ER
  • Made of large + small subunits (assembled in cytoplasm after exiting nucleus)

4.3 Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER) — Manufacturing Factory

TypeRibosomes?Function
Rough ER (RER)Yes (attached)Protein synthesis & secretion
Smooth ER (SER)No (smooth)Synthesis & storage of fats (lipids) and hormones
  • Spreads like a network within cytoplasm
  • Continuous with outer nuclear membrane

4.4 Golgi Apparatus — Packaging & Shipping Centre

  • Stacks of flattened, sac-like structures
  • Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins/lipids into vesicles
  • Acts like cell's post office
  • Sends to: transport, secretion, or lysosome formation
Meet a Scientist: Camillo Golgi

Italian scientist (1898). First observed Golgi apparatus in nerve cells of barn owl using special staining. Many doubted its existence until electron microscopy confirmed it decades later.

4.5 Lysosomes — Clean-up System

  • Single membrane-bound sacs filled with enzymes
  • Break down: unwanted proteins, carbs, fats, damaged cell parts
  • Products reused in cytoplasm
Threads of Curiosity

Human sperm cells contain lysosomal enzymes that break down the outer layer of the egg during fertilisation.

4.6 Mitochondria — Powerhouse of the Cell

FeatureDetail
Outer membraneSmooth, porous
Inner membraneFolded into cristae (increases surface area)
FunctionCellular respiration → breaks down glucose → releases energy
Energy stored asATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) — energy currency of cell
Own DNA & ribosomesYes — can make some of their own proteins

4.7 Plastids — Food Synthesis & Storage (Plant Cells)

TypePigment?Function
ChloroplastsGreen (chlorophyll)Photosynthesis — food synthesis
ChromoplastsYellow, orange, redColour to flowers & fruits (attract pollinators)
LeucoplastsColourlessStorage of starch, oils, proteins (e.g., potato, Colocasia)
  • Chloroplasts have double membrane, stroma (semi-fluid), and disc-shaped structures with chlorophyll
  • Both mitochondria & plastids have own DNA + ribosomes → evolutionary link to bacteria
Renu says all plant parts including roots have plastids. Rohit disagrees saying roots don't perform photosynthesis. Who is correct? Justify.
Show Answer
Rohit is partially correct — roots lack chloroplasts (no photosynthesis). However, roots may contain leucoplasts (colourless plastids) for storing starch. So plastids can be present but not green ones.

4.8 Vacuoles — Storage & Support

FeaturePlant CellsAnimal Cells
SizeOne large central vacuoleSmall, temporary vacuoles
ContentsCell sap (water, minerals, sugars, waste)Temporary storage
FunctionStorage + maintains turgor pressure (firmness)Storage
  • When plant lacks water → vacuole loses water → cells become less firm → plant wilts
What would happen if all mitochondria were removed from a eukaryotic cell?
Show Answer
The cell would not be able to produce ATP (energy) via cellular respiration. Most cellular activities would stop due to lack of energy, and the cell would eventually die.

Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

FeatureProkaryoticEukaryotic
NucleusAbsent (nucleoid region)Present (membrane-bound)
Membrane-bound organellesAbsentPresent
Cell size1–10 μm10–100 μm
Cell typeUsually unicellularUni or multicellular
ExamplesBacteriaPlants, animals, fungi
Ready to Go Beyond: Viruses, viroids, and prions are acellular (not cells). Viruses = genetic material + protein coat. Viroids = no protein coat. Prions = misfolded proteins, no genetic material.
A student observes a cell under a microscope. It has a well-defined nucleus and mitochondria but no cell wall. Is it a plant cell, animal cell, or bacterial cell? Explain.
Show Answer
Animal cell. Well-defined nucleus → not bacteria (prokaryotic). No cell wall → not a plant cell. Therefore it is an animal cell.

Cell Division

6.1 Mitosis

AspectDetail
Produces2 genetically identical daughter cells
PurposeGrowth, repair, maintenance, asexual reproduction
Chromosome numberSame as parent cell
Occurs inAll body cells (somatic cells)

6.2 Meiosis

AspectDetail
Produces4 daughter cells (gametes)
PurposeSexual reproduction, genetic diversity
Chromosome numberHalf of parent cell
Occurs inReproductive organs (testes/ovaries in animals; anthers/ovaries in plants)
ProcessTwo divisions (first = half chromosomes, second = similar to mitosis)
If skin cells started dividing by meiosis instead of mitosis, what would happen to a cut on the skin?
Show Answer

Meiosis produces 4 cells with half the chromosome number. These cells would not be genetically identical to parent skin cells. The cut would not heal properly because:

  • Daughter cells would have abnormal chromosome numbers
  • They would not function as normal skin cells
  • Healing would be impaired/disrupted
Meet a Scientist: Arun Kumar Sharma

Famous Indian botanist. Worked on chromosomes in plants — plant taxonomy, evolution, development. Invented many lab methods to study plant chromosomes. Received Shanti Swarup Bhatnagar award and Padma Bhushan.

Meet a Scientist: Gottlieb Haberlandt (1902)

Austrian botanist. Proposed totipotency — any living plant cell can develop into a complete plant given suitable conditions. Laid foundation for Plant Tissue Culture Technology.

Cell Theory — The Unifying Principle

ScientistYearContribution
Matthias Schleiden (German botanist)1838All plants are made of cells
Theodor Schwann (German zoologist)1839All animals are made of cells
Rudolf Virchow (German scientist)1855New cells form only from pre-existing cells

Classical Cell Theory:

  1. All living organisms are made of one or more cells
  2. Cell is the basic unit of structure and function
  3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells

Cell Growth & Control

ConceptExplanation
Contact inhibitionNormal animal cells stop dividing when they touch neighbours
Cancer cellsLose contact inhibition → divide uncontrollably → tumours (benign or malignant)
Programmed Cell Death (PCD)Genetically regulated cell destruction — e.g., removes cells between fingers during embryo development
Plant growthNo contact inhibition (rigid cell wall); follow different growth pattern
Threads of Curiosity

Cancer cells grow uncontrollably because the normal system of controlled division, aging, and death breaks down.

What mechanism prevents tumour formation in normal human body cells? Can plants develop tumours? Explain.
Show Answer
Contact inhibition — normal animal cells stop dividing when they contact neighbouring cells. Plants can develop tumours because they lack contact inhibition due to rigid cell walls. However, plant tumours follow a different growth pattern.

J. Craig Venter Experiment (Synthetic Biology, 2010)

  • Studied complete DNA of Mycoplasma mycoides
  • Chemically synthesised exact copy of this DNA
  • Removed original DNA from another related bacterium
  • Inserted synthetic DNA → cell grew and divided following synthetic DNA instructions
  • Key insight: DNA controls structure and activities of a cell
  • Important caveat: Only DNA was synthetic; other cell parts came from existing living cell

Cell Sap & Preservation (Competency Context)

  • High salt/sugar creates hypertonic environment
  • Water leaves bacterial/fungal cells by osmosis → they die
  • This is why pickles and murabbas are preserved with salt/sugar
  • Scientific principle: Osmosis + hypertonic solution prevents microbial growth
Bridging Science & Society: Cell culture — growing cells outside body in nutrient-rich medium under sterile conditions. Used for studying cells, producing biochemicals, medicines, vaccines.
