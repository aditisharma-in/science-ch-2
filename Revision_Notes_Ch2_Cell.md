## 1. How to Study Cells?

| Concept | Key Point |
|---------|-----------|
| **Resolution** | Ability of human eye to see two close objects as separate — **0.1 mm** at 25 cm distance |
| **Microscope** | Uses convex lens / combination of lenses (objective + eyepiece) to magnify objects |
| **Light Microscope** | Uses visible light; common in school labs; objective lenses 10X, 40X |
| **Electron Microscope** | Uses beam of electrons instead of light; reveals nanometre-scale details |
| **Cell size estimate** | Formula: Diameter of field (μm) ÷ No. of cells along diameter |

**Magnification = Eyepiece power × Objective lens power**
> e.g., 10X × 10X = **100X total magnification**

**Unit conversion:** 1 mm = 1000 μm | 1 nm = 0.000001 mm

> **Robert Hooke** — First observed cell in 1665 using self-designed microscope (~200–300X). Observed box-like compartments in cork and named them "cells".

---

### 💡 Competency Question (Microscopy)

**Q:** A student sees 20 onion cells across a microscope field of diameter 4000 μm. What is the size of one onion cell? If eyepiece is 10X and objective is 40X, how large will the cell appear?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Size = 4000/20 = 200 μm. Total magnification = 10 × 40 = 400X. Apparent size = 200 × 400 = 80,000 μm = 80 mm.
</details>

## 2. Cell Membrane — Universal Feature of All Cells

- **Thin boundary** surrounding the cell — protects contents
- Also called **plasma membrane**
- **Selectively permeable** — allows some substances, blocks others

### Osmosis
> Movement of water through a selectively permeable membrane from **dilute → concentrated** solution

**Potato experiment (Activity 2.2):**
| Beaker | Solution | Potato piece |
|--------|----------|--------------|
| A | Plain water | Swells (water enters) |
| B | 20% salt/sugar | Shrinks (water leaves) |

### Effect of Solutions on Cells

| Solution | Extracellular vs Intracellular | Effect |
|----------|-------------------------------|--------|
| **Hypotonic** | Outside < Inside concentration | Cell swells (water enters) |
| **Hypertonic** | Outside > Inside concentration | Cell shrinks (water leaves) |
| **Isotonic** | Outside = Inside concentration | No change |

### Structure of Cell Membrane (Fluid-Mosaic Model)
- **Lipid bilayer** — 2 layers of fat molecules (heads outward, tails inward)
- **Proteins** embedded — act as gatekeepers
- **Fluid** — molecules move sideways, flip, rotate
- **Mosaic** — molecules arranged like tiles
- Thickness: **7–10 nm**

---

### 💡 Competency Question (Cell Membrane)

**Q:** A student places a Rhoeo leaf peel in 20% sugar solution. After 30 min, the inner contents shrink but cell shape remains same. Explain why.

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Water moves out due to osmosis (hypertonic solution). The rigid **cell wall** maintains the outer shape while the cell membrane pulls away from it. Animal cells (no cell wall) would shrink entirely.
</details>

## 3. Cell Wall — Outer Covering (Plants, Fungi, Bacteria)

| Feature | Cell Membrane | Cell Wall |
|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Permeability | Selectively permeable | Fully permeable |
| Present in | All cells | Plants, fungi, bacteria only |
| Composition | Lipids + proteins | **Cellulose** (carbohydrate) |
| Function | Gatekeeper | Rigidity, structural support |

- Plant cells: **box-shaped**, regularly arranged
- Animal cells: **irregularly shaped**, flexible (no cell wall)
- Cellulose in our diet acts as **roughage**

---

### 💡 Competency Question (Cell Wall)

**Q:** Predict what would happen if a plant cell's cell wall became as flexible as the cell membrane.

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The plant cell would lose its rigid shape, could not stand upright against wind/rain, leaves and stems would collapse, and the plant would wilt easily. It could not maintain firm structure.
</details>

## 4. Cell Interior — Organelles

All eukaryotic cells have three basic parts:
1. **Plasma membrane** — selectively permeable
2. **Cytoplasm** — semi-fluid, jelly-like substance
3. **Nucleus** — prominent, well-defined

---

### 4.1 Nucleus — House of Coded Instructions

| Part | Function |
|------|----------|
| **Nuclear membrane** | Double-layered, has **pores** for material transfer |
| **Nucleolus** | Dense round body; site of **ribosomal subunit synthesis** |
| **Chromatin** | Entangled thread-like DNA (in non-dividing cell) |
| **Chromosomes** | Rod-shaped (visible during cell division); contain **DNA + proteins** |
| **Genes** | Functional segments of DNA that carry inheritance info |

**From cell to DNA:** Cell → Nucleus → Chromosomes → DNA → Genes

> **Threads of Curiosity:** Mature human RBCs have **no nucleus** (enucleate) — allows more space for haemoglobin. Lifespan ~120 days. Cannot repair/divide.

**Prokaryotic cells:** DNA as single circular molecule in **nucleoid** region (no membrane around it).

---

### 💡 Competency Question (Nucleus)

**Q:** Why do mature mammalian RBCs lack a nucleus? What disadvantage does this cause?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Absence of nucleus provides more space for haemoglobin → transports more oxygen. Disadvantage: cannot repair or divide, so lifespan is short (~120 days).
</details>

---

### 4.2 Ribosomes — Protein Factories

- Sites of **protein synthesis**
- Present freely in cytoplasm OR attached to Rough ER
- Made of large + small subunits (assembled in cytoplasm after exiting nucleus)

---

### 4.3 Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER) — Manufacturing Factory

| Type | Ribosomes? | Function |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Rough ER (RER)** | Yes (attached) | Protein synthesis & secretion |
| **Smooth ER (SER)** | No (smooth) | Synthesis & storage of fats (lipids) and hormones |

- Spreads like a **network** within cytoplasm
- Continuous with **outer nuclear membrane**

---

### 4.4 Golgi Apparatus — Packaging & Shipping Centre

- Stacks of flattened, sac-like structures
- **Modifies, sorts, and packages** proteins/lipids into vesicles
- Acts like cell's **post office**
- Sends to: transport, secretion, or lysosome formation

> **🔬 Meet a Scientist: Camillo Golgi** (Italian, 1898)
> First observed Golgi apparatus in nerve cells of barn owl using special staining. Many doubted its existence until electron microscopy confirmed it decades later.

---

### 4.5 Lysosomes — Clean-up System

- Single membrane-bound sacs filled with **enzymes**
- Break down: unwanted proteins, carbs, fats, damaged cell parts
- Products reused in cytoplasm

> **Threads of Curiosity:** Human sperm cells contain lysosomal enzymes that break down the outer layer of the egg during fertilisation.

---

### 4.6 Mitochondria — Powerhouse of the Cell

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Outer membrane | Smooth, porous |
| Inner membrane | Folded into **cristae** (increases surface area) |
| Function | **Cellular respiration** → breaks down glucose → releases energy |
| Energy stored as | **ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)** — energy currency of cell |
| Own DNA & ribosomes | Yes — can make some of their own proteins |

---

### 4.7 Plastids — Food Synthesis & Storage (Plant Cells)

| Type | Pigment? | Function |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Chloroplasts** | Green (chlorophyll) | **Photosynthesis** — food synthesis |
| **Chromoplasts** | Yellow, orange, red | **Colour** to flowers & fruits (attract pollinators) |
| **Leucoplasts** | Colourless | **Storage** of starch, oils, proteins (e.g., potato, Colocasia) |

- Chloroplasts have **double membrane**, stroma (semi-fluid), and disc-shaped structures with chlorophyll
- Both mitochondria & plastids have **own DNA + ribosomes** → evolutionary link to bacteria

---

### 💡 Competency Question (Plastids)

**Q:** Renu says all plant parts including roots have plastids. Rohit disagrees saying roots don't perform photosynthesis. Who is correct? Justify.

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Rohit is partially correct — roots lack **chloroplasts** (no photosynthesis). However, roots may contain **leucoplasts** (colourless plastids) for storing starch. So plastids can be present but not green ones.
</details>

---

### 4.8 Vacuoles — Storage & Support

| Feature | Plant Cells | Animal Cells |
|---------|-------------|--------------|
| Size | One **large central vacuole** | Small, temporary vacuoles |
| Contents | **Cell sap** (water, minerals, sugars, waste) | Temporary storage |
| Function | Storage + maintains **turgor pressure** (firmness) | Storage |

- When plant lacks water → vacuole loses water → cells become less firm → **plant wilts**

---

### 💡 Competency Question (Mitochondria & Vacuoles)

**Q:** What would happen if all mitochondria were removed from a eukaryotic cell?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The cell would not be able to produce ATP (energy) via cellular respiration. Most cellular activities would stop due to lack of energy, and the cell would eventually die.
</details>

## 5. Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

| Feature | Prokaryotic | Eukaryotic |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| Nucleus | **Absent** (nucleoid region) | **Present** (membrane-bound) |
| Membrane-bound organelles | **Absent** | **Present** |
| Cell size | **1–10 μm** | **10–100 μm** |
| Cell type | Usually unicellular | Uni or multicellular |
| Examples | Bacteria | Plants, animals, fungi |

> **Ready to Go Beyond:** Viruses, viroids, and prions are **acellular** (not cells). Viruses = genetic material + protein coat. Viroids = no protein coat. Prions = misfolded proteins, no genetic material.

---

### 💡 Competency Question (Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic)

**Q:** A student observes a cell under a microscope. It has a well-defined nucleus and mitochondria but no cell wall. Is it a plant cell, animal cell, or bacterial cell? Explain.

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Animal cell.** Well-defined nucleus → not bacteria (prokaryotic). No cell wall → not a plant cell. Therefore it is an animal cell.
</details>

## 6. Cell Division

### 6.1 Mitosis

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Produces | **2 genetically identical daughter cells** |
| Purpose | Growth, repair, maintenance, asexual reproduction |
| Chromosome number | **Same** as parent cell |
| Occurs in | All body cells (somatic cells) |

### 6.2 Meiosis

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Produces | **4 daughter cells** (gametes) |
| Purpose | Sexual reproduction, genetic diversity |
| Chromosome number | **Half** of parent cell |
| Occurs in | Reproductive organs (testes/ovaries in animals; anthers/ovaries in plants) |
| Process | **Two divisions** (first = half chromosomes, second = similar to mitosis) |

---

### 💡 Competency Question (Cell Division)

**Q:** If skin cells started dividing by meiosis instead of mitosis, what would happen to a cut on the skin?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Meiosis produces 4 cells with **half the chromosome number**. These cells would not be genetically identical to parent skin cells. The cut would not heal properly because:
- Daughter cells would have abnormal chromosome numbers
- They would not function as normal skin cells
- Healing would be impaired/disrupted

</details>

## 7. Cell Theory — The Unifying Principle

| Scientist | Year | Contribution |
|-----------|------|--------------|
| **Matthias Schleiden** (German botanist) | 1838 | All **plants** are made of cells |
| **Theodor Schwann** (German zoologist) | 1839 | All **animals** are made of cells |
| **Rudolf Virchow** (German scientist) | 1855 | New cells form **only from pre-existing cells** |

**Classical Cell Theory:**
1. All living organisms are made of **one or more cells**
2. Cell is the **basic unit** of structure and function
3. All cells arise from **pre-existing cells**

## 8. Cell Growth & Control

| Concept | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Contact inhibition** | Normal animal cells stop dividing when they touch neighbours |
| **Cancer cells** | Lose contact inhibition → divide uncontrollably → **tumours** (benign or malignant) |
| **Programmed Cell Death (PCD)** | Genetically regulated cell destruction — e.g., removes cells between fingers during embryo development |
| **Plant growth** | No contact inhibition (rigid cell wall); follow different growth pattern |

> **Threads of Curiosity:** Cancer cells grow uncontrollably because the normal system of controlled division, aging, and death breaks down.

---

### 💡 Competency Question (Cell Theory & Growth)

**Q:** What mechanism prevents tumour formation in normal human body cells? Can plants develop tumours? Explain.

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Contact inhibition** — normal animal cells stop dividing when they contact neighbouring cells. Plants **can** develop tumours because they lack contact inhibition due to rigid cell walls. However, plant tumours follow a different growth pattern.
</details>

## 📌 J. Craig Venter Experiment (Synthetic Biology, 2010)

- Studied complete DNA of *Mycoplasma mycoides*
- **Chemically synthesised** exact copy of this DNA
- Removed original DNA from another related bacterium
- Inserted **synthetic DNA** → cell grew and divided following synthetic DNA instructions
- **Key insight:** DNA controls structure and activities of a cell
- **Important caveat:** Only DNA was synthetic; other cell parts came from existing living cell

## 📌 Cell Sap & Preservation (Competency Context)

- **High salt/sugar** creates **hypertonic environment**
- Water leaves bacterial/fungal cells by **osmosis** → they die
- This is why pickles and murabbas are preserved with salt/sugar
- Scientific principle: **Osmosis + hypertonic solution prevents microbial growth**
